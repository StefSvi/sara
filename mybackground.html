My Background

For many years, my personal life was dominated by raising six children, who are now 24, 22, 20, 17, 15 and 13 years old. From 1995 until 2008 I worked as a journalist, first as an editor, and eventually as a weekly columnist for The New York Sun, covering parenting and child rearing issues. I was a regular commentor on CBS Morning News and Fox News and lectured at local schools.


In 2008, I joined my father in his work at The Steinhardt Foundation for Jewish Life, where I helped to start entrepreneurial organizations such as Hebrew Public and OneTable. In this period, I learned firsthand about organizational structures, conflicts, and mergers. As board chair of numerous non-profit organizations and schools, I learned to listen, embrace my greatest critics, prioritize organizational goals, and work collaboratively with staff and board members. I learned when to lead and when to follow. I also learned about the specific joys and challenges of working in a family setting. In 2015, after 20 years of marriage, I made the difficult decision to get divorced. With six children, this period of my life was agonizing. I learned quite a bit about conflict and harnessing creativity during this difficult period. I worked with mediators, lawyers, and therapists and learned firsthand about the benefits and drawbacks of each.


In 2020, I began an MS in Negotiation and Conflict Resolution at Columbia University. I studied conflict and power theories, dynamical systems theory, distributive bargaining, integrative negotiation, intractable conflict, and mediation. I learned about the outsized role emotions play in conflict. Conflict is exhausting and confusing, and one of the advantages of the mediation process is that it allows people to sort through their thinking. I was inspired by social psychologist Evelin Lindner, an expert in emotional awareness and regulation.

She wrote: We have to learn to move back and forth, get into the othersâ€™ perspective and feelings, and then step back into our own perspective. We must learn to stay calm and use frustration creatively with imagination and inspiration. For that we need to nurture qualities of curiosity, courage, and patience in ourselves and in others.

We need to learn to start with recognizing each other, and then move toward reconciliation and resolution. For some people this is more natural than for others. But everyone can grow.

When I finished the degree in 2022, I noticed there was a gaping hole in the field in the services offered to high-net-worth families. Lawyers and therapists are clamoring to offer their services to this clientele, who are often struggling to navigate estate planning, intergenerational transfers of wealth, philanthropic foundations and ventures, high-worth family businesses, and other aspects of life that are unique to wealthy families. In my own personal experiences, lawyers are not always incentivized to work to resolve conflict quickly and constructively, and therapists have very little training in conflict resolution.

In 2023, I opened a practice dedicated to serving all people, but particularly families like my own, that have had the blessing and sometimes curse of extraordinary wealth. My aim is to help clients navigate through conflict, but also to transfer the kind of listening, collaborating, and negotiating skills that result in independence from needing intervention in the future.
